The whales of Kamay

Humpback whales are on the move every year, heading south for... (Read more)

Humpback whales are on the move every year, heading south for the summer to feed in polar waters and north for the winter to mate, give birth, and socialize in the tropics. On the east coast of Australia, they’re a regular sight along the Sydney coastline. Pregnant mothers show up around May and June as they migrate north, and then return from September to October with their newborn calves, making their way back to Antarctica. If you’re in Sydney during migration season, one of the best places to watch them from land is the cliffs at Kamay Botany Bay National Park. It’s hard to beat the view—whales breaching and tail-slapping against the backdrop of open ocean, all from a spot where you’re comfortably perched on solid ground.

The wild hucul of Hungary

The wild Hucul horses of of Aggtelek National Park in Hungary... (Read more)

The wild Hucul horses of Aggtelek National Park in Hungary are a fascinating link to the past, embodying the tough, no-nonsense resilience of the Carpathian region. Often called "the little horse of the mountains," these sturdy animals are descendants of ancient breeds once relied upon for work and transport in harsh mountain terrain. In Jósvafő, within the heart of Aggtelek National Park, a special reserve keeps this tradition alive. Here, the Hucul horses live in a semi-wild state, roaming freely across meadows and wooded hills. They’re smart, tough, and sure-footed—perfectly adapted to the rugged landscape they call home. Beyond being a conservation success, these horses are part of Hungary’s cultural heritage and contribute to the park’s ecosystem by naturally grazing the land. It’s a glimpse into history, wrapped up in the beauty of nature.

Lady Musgrave Island

Lady Musgrave Island is hands down one of my favorite spots on the Great Barrier Reef. Tucked away in the Capricornia Cays National Park, it’s a little slice of paradise. Its (almost) fully enclosed lagoon is one of the few in the region, providing a safe haven for all kinds of marine life. In the middle of it all is a tiny sand cay, shaded by a lush Pisonia forest. This is prime real estate for noddy terns, wedge-tailed shearwaters, and other seabirds that call the canopy home. On the beaches, sea turtles haul themselves ashore to nest, while just offshore, the surrounding coral reef is alive with fish, invertebrates, and endless underwater activity. Manta rays and humpback whales also swing by during their migrations, making it even more special. It’s a place where nature really puts on a show.

Mallorca – island of 1000 faces

Mallorca, sitting in the heart of the Mediterranean Sea... (Read more)

Mallorca, sitting in the heart of the Mediterranean Sea just off the coast of Barcelona and Valencia, is an island that seems to have it all. From its pristine beaches and rugged cliffs to towering mountains, ancient olive groves, and picturesque villages, it’s a place where nature, history, and culture collide. The island’s capital, Palma, is a perfect mix of old-world charm and modern energy. Its Gothic cathedral, La Seu, dominates the skyline, while the old town invites you to wander through its cobblestone streets, discover hidden courtyards, and soak up the lively atmosphere of its plazas.

Lady Elliott Island

Lady Elliott Island sits at the southernmost tip of the Great Barrier Reef... (Read more)

Lady Elliott Island sits at the southernmost tip of the Great Barrier Reef, and it’s pretty special. Unlike places like Lady Musgrave, it doesn’t have a lagoon. Instead, you’ve got coral reefs starting right at the beach—no need to go far to see the action. You can snorkel straight off the shore and find turtles, reef sharks, even humpback whales and manta rays, i the winter months. On land, it’s mostly Pisonia forests with noddy terns and shearwaters nesting wherever they can.
